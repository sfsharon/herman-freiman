## 6. Tattoo Question Analysis

**Conclusion: Herman Freiman almost certainly did NOT have a concentration camp tattoo.**

**Evidence and Reasoning:**

- **Tattoos were Auschwitz-specific:** The practice of tattooing prisoners was unique to the Auschwitz concentration camp complex. Other camps and ghettos did not tattoo prisoners.
- **Herman was in ZAL, not KZ:** Documents consistently show 'Gh. u. ZAL Boryslaw' (Ghetto and Zwangsarbeitslager/Forced Labor Camp Boryslaw). A ZAL was a forced labor facility, not a concentration camp (Konzentrationslager/KZ).
- **No KZ documentation:** The ITS Certificate explicitly states: 'Über die Inhaftierung liegen keine Unterlagen vor' (No documents exist regarding concentration camp incarceration).
- **Local labor camp context:** The Boryslaw ZAL was associated with the local oil industry. Workers were kept locally for forced labor rather than being transported to Auschwitz.
- **Survival factors:** His profession as a tailor (Schneider) would have been valuable to the Germans, likely keeping him in local forced labor rather than deportation to extermination camps.

## 9. Historical Context: Jewish Boryslaw

Information compiled from Yad Vashem's Pinkas HaKehillot (Encyclopedia of Jewish Communities in Poland):

### 9.1 Pre-War Jewish Community

- Boryslaw (Boryslav) was a major oil-producing center in Eastern Galicia
- Jewish population: approximately 13,000–14,000 (about 40% of the city)
- Active Jewish community with synagogues, schools, and cultural institutions
- Many Jews worked in the oil industry and related trades

### 9.2 Holocaust Period

- German occupation began July 1, 1941
- Multiple 'Aktionen' (roundups) conducted with mass shootings and deportations
- Jews were confined to ghetto and forced labor camps serving the oil industry
- Deportations primarily to Bełżec extermination camp
- Workers in essential industries (like tailors) sometimes avoided initial deportations
- Final liquidation actions in 1943–1944
- Only approximately 400–600 Jews from Boryslaw survived the Holocaust
