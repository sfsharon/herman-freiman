### The Jewish settlement from its beginnings until 1919

Until the 1840s, Borysław was a village at the foot of the Carpathian Mountains. After ozokerite (earth-wax) and petroleum were discovered in and around the town, the settlement developed rapidly, and its Jewish population grew especially. In the second half of the 19th century, oil refining began. Borysław became the center of oil and oil products in the Austrian Empire. In the period between the two world wars, Borysław and its immediate vicinity supplied about 75% of Poland's oil consumption. In those same years, the extraction of natural gas also began, along with its use for industrial and domestic purposes. Between 1939–1941, and after the German occupation, in 1944, the Soviets worked to increase oil extraction in Borysław and to turn it into an important industrial center.

At the beginning of the 19th century there was a small Jewish community in the village of Borysław. Until 1860, Jews from surrounding settlements would come to Borysław to work in the extraction of ozokerite and oil, but most did not settle in the place permanently. By the end of the 1860s there was already a permanent community in Borysław with a central synagogue and several study houses (*batei midrash*). However, the Jewish community in Borysław was not independent but was tied to the community council of Drohobycz. As the settlement grew in numbers and in economic importance, the Jews of Borysław made efforts to become an independent community. In 1908 they petitioned the Austrian authorities to recognize them as a separate community, but owing to the vigorous protest of the Drohobycz community council, the request was rejected.

Finally, on 18 April 1928, the Polish authorities gave their consent to establish an independent community in Borysław. The Jews of Borysław had laid the foundations for independent communal life already at the end of the 19th century. In 1886 a Jewish cemetery was consecrated in the town, on a plot donated by the Lindenbaum family, which was expanded in 1913. In addition, the Jews of Borysław owned a central bathhouse that was at the community's disposal. The religious institutions, too, maintained a large measure of independence. In the late 1880s, Rabbi Eliyahu Ḥarav served as the rabbi of Borysław. At the end of the 19th century, Rabbi Shmuel Yitzchak Perler held the rabbinic seat, and at the beginning of the 20th century, Rabbi Meshulam Rokeach. From 1897 a *Bikur Cholim* (visiting-the-sick) society operated there. At the same time there were also a *Hachnasat Kallah* society (providing for brides), a *Linat Tzedek* society, and other organizations, which extended aid to the needy and cared for orphans and widows.

The Jews were among the pioneers of the oil industry in Borysław. The first attempts to extract oil in the Borysław area were made in the 1820s by the Jew Hecker, but without practical results. A few years later, Abraham Schreiner, a Jew from Borysław, also worked toward the industrial exploitation of the natural resources around the town. He was a producer of wagon-grease, and in the course of producing the grease from crude oil he arrived at the principle of oil refining. The liquids that Schreiner distilled from the oil were brought to a Polish pharmacist in Lwów, Łukasiewicz, who examined them and perfected the oil-refining process. Following this discovery, oil refineries were established in 1858 in Borysław and in Drohobycz, and this was a turning point in the development of the oil industry in the region.

Until 1880, about 2,500 Jewish men and about 500 Jewish women were employed in the oil industry in Borysław, but most of them did not reside in the town permanently. These were Jews of Drohobycz, Bolechów, Skole and other settlements in the area, who would return to their places of residence.

In the 1860s and 1870s, the extraction of oil, its refining, and the extraction and processing of ozokerite in Borysław and its immediate vicinity were largely in Jewish hands. This development also led to a great increase in the number of Jews employed in drilling oil wells and in transporting the mined ozokerite. Jews mainly served as supervisors at the plants and the drilling sites. During that period a large group of Jews also grew, who worked as cashiers or paymasters at all the oil enterprises and were responsible for making the payments to the thousands of wage laborers who came to Borysław from the whole surrounding area. These workers, who were called *kasirers*, had a study house of their own called the "Kasirer Beit Midrash," which existed until 1914.

Small Jewish-owned refineries were established in Borysław, among them: "Liberman & Marmelstein," "Schützmann & Co.," and "A.G. — Borysław," whose shares were held by the Hartenstein family.

In the late 1890s and the beginning of the 20th century, changes occurred in the production methods and in the ownership of the oil fields. The Vienna "Länderbank" and one of the central banks in Galicia bought up small plots of land on which oil wells were located and dispossessed their owners. Modern production methods were also introduced, using steam and electricity. The small producers could not withstand the competition and were forced to sell their assets. As a result of these changes, Jewish workers were dispossessed of their work, and broad strata of the Borysław community fell into a severe economic crisis. The banks and the new owners of a considerable portion of the oil wells preferred Christian workers, claiming that the Jewish workers were unskilled. But this argument did not stand the test of reality, since the Jews had reached a high degree of expertise in all branches of the oil industry, and an antisemitic approach was evident here. In 1899, 700 Jews were dismissed from their work. That same year, 50 Jewish supervisors and work-managers were required to be examined in the Polish language; only 3 of them passed the examination, and the rest were dismissed.

In order to assist the Jewish families who had been economically impoverished by this crisis and had lost their source of livelihood, relief committees were established in all the surrounding communities. The central Jewish organizations, such as ICA (the Jewish Colonization Association), the Alliance Israélite Universelle, and the "Galizischer Hilfsverein," also extended special aid at that time to the Jews of Borysław. In 1898 Baroness Hirsch donated a large sum to support 240 Jewish families in the town. Following the crisis, the flow of emigration from Borysław to the United States increased, and public institutions even financed the travel expenses of the needy. Thus, for example, 40 families emigrated from Borysław in the autumn of 1898. The Zionist organization in Galicia, "Ahavat Zion," also took note of the plight of the Jews of Borysław and discussed a plan to bring Jewish families from the town to the Land of Israel.

In order to withstand the harsh competition from the Vienna "Länderbank," the Jewish oil-well owners, especially the wealthier among them, also organized themselves into companies and even established a Jewish bank to consolidate their enterprises. However, even these measures did not prevent the process by which medium and small Jewish enterprises were harmed. Nevertheless, the Jews did not give up their share in the oil industry and continued to fill an important role in it.

In Borysław and its immediate vicinity, large quantities of natural gas were found, discovered together with the oil extraction. The Jews' share was prominent here too, in the extraction of the gas and its utilization. In laying networks of pipes to utilize the gas, the contractors Dienstag and Motl Pomeranz excelled. In 1910 the engineer Joachim Hausmann began to perfect the methods for measuring the quantities of gas.

Dr. Freund of Borysław began producing gasoline from natural gas. At first this process was carried out by a primitive method, but on the eve of the First World War the Jewish engineers Werstelman and Landes of Borysław perfected the methods, thereby laying the foundations for accelerated industrial development in this branch.

The crude oil flowed from the wells in large quantities, and it was not always possible to collect all of the material in the facilities used for this purpose. Indeed, the crude oil floated in every channel and river in the town and its surroundings. A large group of Jews engaged in collecting this oil, and they were called *łebaks*. This was a unique social-economic phenomenon among the members of the Borysław community. Most of them were older and elderly Jews, who, with a broom made of reeds, raked the oil into buckets and, after special processing, sold it for heating purposes. In this way they barely earned their living.

With the increase in output, a solution had to be found for storing the crude oil, and for this purpose the company "Petrolea" was established in Borysław; many Jews were counted among its workers and managers.

In 1914, about 750 Jews were employed as wage workers in the oil industry in Borysław.

In 1887 the first "Hovevei Zion" society was founded in Borysław, and shortly after its founding it had 112 members. Following pressure exerted by the Orthodox, the society was disbanded. Attempts to establish a new Zionist society were renewed in 1891, at the initiative of the Hebrew writer Zvi Eliezer Teller, and indeed in 1892 the Bnei Zion organization was founded. Alongside the organization, a library and reading hall were established. In 1894 a Zionist society, Achdut, was organized, with about 300 members. In 1897 the Ahavat Zion organization operated there, with a club beside it where Hebrew courses were also held. From 1902 a town branch of the Zionist Organization began its activity. In 1911 a Mizrachi branch was founded. In 1908 the local Po'alei Zion branch opened extensive activity among Jewish workers and competed for influence over them with the socialist circles. At the end of the 19th century, Jewish workers' clubs — "Forverts," "Glaykhheyt" and "Briderlekhkeyt" — were established in Borysław; these were under the influence of the PPS. The circles that gathered around these clubs conducted activity to defend the rights of the Jewish workers, especially during the crisis and the great unemployment that, as noted, struck the body of workers in the oil industry at the end of the 19th century.

In 1908 a considerable portion of the activists in the aforementioned organizations distanced themselves from the PPS and made contact with the ŻPS — the Jewish Socialist Party. At the initiative of the ŻPS branch in Borysław, workers raised demands for an 8-hour workday and for the improvement of working conditions. Before the First World War, the number of young Jews who worked in commercial houses and shops grew, and these established a "Defense Committee" for apprentices and shop assistants.

In 1890 a Jewish public elementary school was founded in Borysław by the Vienna Alliance Israélite Universelle. 353 pupils studied in it. In 1896 the number of pupils in it grew to 403, and in 1901 to 554. Later this school was attached to the educational network founded by Baron Hirsch.

In 1893 a supplementary Hebrew school was established for the children who studied in general schools. At the beginning of the 20th century this school was affiliated with the "Safa Berura" network of schools. In 1906 it had 47 boys and 56 girls in 3 classes. In 1907 the institution closed, but it renewed its activity in 1908.

In 1910 a modern Hebrew school was inaugurated in the town, and about 300 pupils enrolled in it, but in the first stage 6 classes were opened for about 150 pupils. The school was supported by the community and the municipal council. Children from poor families were given hot meals and clothing. In 1912 the "Center of Hebrew Teachers in Galicia" was located in Borysław.

Two prominent figures in the field of Hebrew and Yiddish literature in Galicia were active in the educational field in Borysław: Yitzchak Even and Zvi Eliezer Teller. Yitzchak Even (1861–1925), born in Rozwadów, lived in Borysław for many years, earned his living from teaching, and published articles in "Machzikei Hadat." From 1893 he was a regular contributor to "Hamagid," where he published articles, translations and stories. In Borysław he taught Hebrew and worked to instill the Zionist idea among young Jews. In 1895 he moved to Lwów and founded a Hebrew school there. In 1909 he emigrated to America and published articles and Hasidic stories in the Yiddish press. Zvi Eliezer Teller (1840–1920), born in Złoczów, came to Borysław in 1892, worked there as a Hebrew teacher, and gathered around him the circles of Jewish maskilim (enlightened intellectuals) in the town. Among his works: a translation into Hebrew of Lessing's play "Die Juden" (The Jews); a collection of poems titled "Siftei Renanot" (Drohobycz 1892); a work of contemplative thought — "Kol Elohim"; stories — "Mistarim," "Nachalat Avot," "Shlumim," "Osher Shamur," "Ziona," and others. A considerable portion of these works were published in Drohobycz and Lwów.

The Hebrew writer Michal Berkowicz (1865–1935) was born in Borysław. He was one of the leaders of the Zionist society "Zion" in Galicia; he translated Herzl's "Der Judenstaat" (The Jewish State) and served as Herzl's Hebrew secretary. He was among the founders of the weekly "Der Yid" in Kraków and among the prominent contributors to "Die Welt"; he engaged in Semitic linguistics and published studies and a commentary on Targum Onkelos in "Monumenta Judaica" in Vienna; he translated Herzl's Zionist writings into Hebrew, and he published the writings of the Jewish socialist Aaron Lieberman.

Among the maskilim born in Borysław, Avigdor Marmelstein (1850–1925) should be mentioned. In 1875 he was among the founders of the Society for the Settlement of the Land of Israel in Galicia. He was a Hebrew teacher in Przemyśl, and there, in the years 1879–1881, he took part in editing the Hebrew bi-weekly "Ha'ohev Amo ve'Eretz Moladto" (One Who Loves His People and His Homeland). From 1882 he was one of the prominent activists in the field of Jewish education in Galicia. Marmelstein distinguished himself as one of the first Hebrew teachers to teach by the "Hebrew in Hebrew" method.

After the First World War broke out and the Russian army advanced westward at the end of August 1914, hundreds of Jews fled the town, especially the wealthy among them. Many found refuge in Kraków, and some even wandered as far as Vienna. During the period of the town's occupation by the Russians, from September 1914 until June 1915, the Jewish population suffered physical assaults by the soldiers and economic harassment. With the return of the Austrians, some of the Jewish refugees returned to Borysław as well. In the spring of 1919, the authorities of the West Ukrainian People's Republic increased the burden on the Jewish population of the town, especially in the economic sphere; the tax burden grew, and special contributions were also collected.

### Between the two world wars

The First World War led to a slowing of economic activity in the town and deepened the economic crisis among the Jews. In 1919, about 40% of the community members were in need of *kimcha de'pischa* (Passover charity). At the beginning of the 1920s, public kitchens were established that distributed hundreds of meals a day.

In 1921 the Jewish wage workers were about 18% of all the wage workers in the town — 16.4% among the workers in the oil plants. In 1921 there were 1,218 Jewish workers in the oil industry, according to the following occupational breakdown:

Wholesale and retail commerce in Borysław was, for the most part, in Jewish hands, although the Jewish merchants were in fierce competition with the Polish economic elements, who enjoyed support and preferential credit from the authorities. The body of Jewish merchants in the years 1919–1939 was organized in 3 associations: the "Association of Merchants and Industrialists," the "Association of Jewish Merchants," and the "Association of Small Merchants." In 1936 an attempt was made to unite these associations into a single organization. The activities of the Jewish merchants' associations in the town were conducted in the "Jewish Merchant's House." In 1929 a "Cooperative Credit Bank" was founded, which assisted the merchants. The "Rodfei Tzedek" society also operated in the town, extending aid to merchants and craftsmen in distress. Among the Jewish craftsmen, some organized at the beginning of the 1920s in the "Po'alei Yeshu'ot" society, and from 1926 in "Yad Charutzim." That year 150 members were registered in it. Alongside "Yad Charutzim" there were a sick fund and a free-loan fund (*gemach*). The scope of the work of the free-loan funds in Borysław, which were supported by the Joint (JDC), can be learned from these figures:

However, all of the aforementioned means of aid and mutual aid eased the situation of the Jews of Borysław only slightly. Unemployment, especially in the 1930s, encompassed many hundreds of breadwinners; particularly hard hit were wage workers and small merchants who were forced to close their businesses. In 1931 there were Jews in the town who attacked wagons that carried bread to the shops. The Jews were discriminated against by the municipality in the matter of their employment in relief works. Out of 1,200 people employed in public works in 1936, only 60 of them were Jews.

In Borysław there were branches of Agudat Israel and of the Bund, which arose on the basis of the ŻPS. It is not possible to estimate precisely the share of the Jews among the members of the illegal Communist Party; but according to an estimate that was widespread among the Jews of Borysław, and according to the number of Jews accused by the Polish authorities of communist activity — brought to trial, sentenced and imprisoned — their number was very prominent. Among the communist activists in Borysław was Marek Ratner, a medical student at the University of Prague. He was sentenced to 6 years' imprisonment, was released by the Soviets in 1939, and was appointed deputy head of the municipal council in 1939–1941.

When the First World War ended, the branch of the Zionist Organization, which, as noted, was founded at the beginning of the century, became a central factor in spreading the Zionist idea among the Jews of the town. Leon Schützmann headed the Zionist Organization. In 1923 a Hitachdut branch was founded. At the beginning of the 1930s its members united with Po'alei Zion in the town. In 1934, 180 members were registered in this united party. The General Zionists numbered about 150 members at the beginning of the 1930s. The activity of Mizrachi, which had weakened in the 1920s, was strengthened in 1930. A branch of the Revisionist movement operated in the town during the period under discussion. The women, organized in WIZO, conducted extensive work in the areas of mutual aid, education and culture. There also existed in Borysław Zionist societies such as "Zionist Professionals" and the "Theodor Herzl" society.

In Borysław there was a wide range of youth movements. The Hashomer Hatzair "nest" (*ken*) began its activity already at the start of the 1920s; in 1920 the authorities banned the organization's activity for a time, claiming that it spread ideas hostile to the state. In 1930 a nest of Hanoar Hatzioni was founded. There were also in the place branches of Achva, Akiba, Betar, Gordonia, Hechalutz, and the General Zionist Hechalutz. The strength of the Zionist organizations in Borysław can be learned from the results of the elections to the Zionist Congresses presented above.

Most of the Jewish children in Borysław between the two world wars studied in general schools, even though in some of them a harsh antisemitic atmosphere prevailed among non-Jewish teachers and pupils. Jewish "religious studies" in the general schools gradually contracted. In 1936, about 1,500 Jewish children studied in 10 general elementary schools in the town, and only 3 teachers were employed there in teaching religion. That same year it was reported that in the suburbs of Borysław — Tustanowice, Mraźnica and Bania Kotowska — no religion lessons at all were held in the general elementary schools, despite the large concentration of Jewish children there. In the "Talmud Torah," which had been founded back in 1911, in 1922 there were 652 pupils aged 5 to 14, in about 15 classes. There also existed in the place a Beit Yaakov school. A Hebrew school, which renewed its activity after the war, affiliated with the "Tarbut" network. This school, in cooperation with the "Theodor Herzl" society, also organized Hebrew courses for adults. Hebrew lessons were also given in the community's orphanage and within the framework of all the Zionist organizations.

The Jewish community and Jewish women's circles in the town organized a series of kindergartens in which the children also received a Jewish education. Because of the severe economic crisis that struck considerable portions of the Jewish population in the years between the two world wars, the Jewish educational institutions also became an important instrument in extending material aid to pupils. In the educational institutions, hundreds of hot meals were distributed daily, as well as clothing and books and study supplies.

The "Leon Reich Jewish House" served as a center for Jewish cultural activity in the town. Concerts and theater performances by troupes that came to Borysław from all over Poland were held in it. During that period, performances of the Jacob Gordin Drama Circle, directed by the Drohobycz poet Tuvia Adler, were held in the town. Alongside the "Jewish House," an amateur orchestra, a choir, and a public library of 7,000 volumes operated. In the Gordonia nest and in the Po'alei Zion branch there were drama circles. Alongside the "Theodor Herzl" society there were a reading room and a library.

The Jewish sports association "Kadima" was founded in 1921 and included a soccer team, a gymnastics section (about 100 members), shooting clubs, table tennis (more than 100 youths), skiing, and light athletics. "Kadima" also had an orchestra. On behalf of "Kadima," activities were organized for the Maccabi village in the Land of Israel. The sports clubs "Hapoel" and "Betar" also existed in Borysław.

From the end of the First World War, the leaders of the Jewish settlement in Borysław worked to detach it from the Drohobycz community and to turn it into an independent community. Leon Kaufmann headed this struggle, gathering around him all the representatives of Borysław in the Drohobycz community council. In 1924 a vigorous demand was directed to the district authorities for separation from the Drohobycz community, but at this stage the demand was rejected. That year the members of the Borysław community council demanded the opening of a new cemetery and a Jewish hospital in the town. Among the people of Borysław and its leaders, a feeling was widespread that they were being shortchanged in the area of developing communal services because of their affiliation with the council in Drohobycz.

As noted, only on 8 April 1928 did the Polish authorities approve the establishment of an independent community in Borysław. L. Kaufmann headed the community council, and Leon Schützmann headed the council secretariat. In the elections to the community council in 1930, the various Zionist streams won 10 of the 12 seats. In the 1934 elections, Leon Schützmann was elected chairman of the council and Eliyahu Klinghofer his deputy. Leon Schützmann held this position until the outbreak of the Second World War.

The Borysław community council worked to maintain the essential communal services, but encountered financing difficulties. In 1936 the community's budget amounted to 107,020 złoty, and in 1937 to 98,105 złoty. The community council's income was in constant decline — precisely at a time when the community's needs, especially in the area of aid to strata in distress, were steadily growing.

The community council directly operated a number of public institutions, among them the orphanage, where about 60 children were kept under boarding conditions. In addition, about 60 more children, who studied there during the day and received free meals, benefited from the orphanage's services. In 1936 the orphanage provided more than 30,000 lunches and about 10,000 breakfasts and dinners. In the summer months the community council ran summer camps for children of needy families, especially for the children of the unemployed. In the years 1934–1938, between 150 and 200 children took part in these camps each year.

In extending material aid to the needy, the societies "Beit Lechem," "Bikur Cholim," "Hashgacha" and the "Social Club" were active in Borysław. Jewish women distinguished themselves in the work of these societies, holding fundraising drives, collecting and distributing clothing, opening public kitchens, and arranging medical aid. In the area of public health, the "TOZ" branch did much in the town; under its auspices a station for combating tuberculosis and a mother-and-child station were opened, and medical aid was also provided to the pupils of the Talmud Torah and of Beit Yaakov.

In the 1930s, Rabbi David Schreiber held the rabbinic seat in Borysław. After his death in 1936, Rabbi Jacob Avigdor served — who, from the beginning of the 1920s until the Second World War, was the rabbi of Drohobycz and of the entire region.

The Jews endeavored to secure proper representation for themselves in the municipal council. In the 1927 elections, a united Jewish list ran, except for the Bund, and 19 Jews were elected to the council alongside 22 Poles and 7 Ukrainians. In the 1939 municipal council elections, the Jews integrated into two mixed lists: the "Non-Party" list won a majority — 23 representatives, including 11 Jews. Before the elections, the Poles — the leaders of this list — promised the post of deputy mayor to the Jews, but after the elections they did not keep this promise.

In the 1930s, manifestations of open antisemitism, bordering on pogroms, intensified against the Jews of Borysław. Attacks multiplied on Jewish merchants who visited the surrounding villages on business. In 1936 a wave of assemblies organized by the Endeks (National Democrats) swept through, accompanied by acts of violence. The Jews prepared to repel these attacks, and the young among them responded with force to these assaults.

### The Second World War

A few days after the war broke out, in September 1939, masses of refugees from western Poland, fleeing the advancing Germans, began flowing through Borysław in the direction of the Romanian border. Among the refugees were many Jews, and the local community extended aid to those who decided, for the time being, to remain in the town, and also to those refugees who delayed for a short stay in the place and then continued their flight.

On 12 September 1939, units of the German army reached Borysław, and the Jewish population became a target for their persecutions. German soldiers seized Jews for labor, abused them, and looted Jewish property. Encouraged by the presence of the Germans, the local Ukrainian nationalists also began to organize riots against the Jews. In the suburbs of the town, where the Jews lived among a dense Ukrainian population, there were cases of physical assaults on members of the community. On the eve of Yom Kippur, which fell on 22 September 1939, reports reached the Jews of Ukrainian peasants from the surrounding villages organizing to carry out a pogrom against the Jews of Borysław, and it is believed that only the appearance of Red Army units at the conclusion of Yom Kippur prevented these planned riots. Two days after Yom Kippur the Germans left the town, and the period of Soviet rule began.

The Jewish population received the Soviets with feelings of relief, since this put an end to the terrors of the Nazi occupation. As part of the nationalization policy, oil wells, refineries and other Jewish-owned plants were nationalized. The community institutions ceased to function, and the Jewish parties were dispersed. In the spring of 1940, Jewish public activists and activists of the Zionist parties and of the Bund were arrested and exiled into the interior of the Soviet Union. Among the exiles was also Leon Schützmann, the former chairman of the community council and a local Zionist leader.

Local Jewish communists filled prominent roles in the municipal administration, in industry, and in the education system. In 1940 a process began of integrating local Ukrainians and Soviet functionaries into key positions; nevertheless, a considerable number of the Jewish communists of Borysław continued to fill important positions in the town. A considerable portion of the Jewish refugees from western Poland refused to accept Soviet citizenship, and at the end of June 1940 they were exiled into the interior of the Soviet Union.

At the beginning of 1940, the Soviet authorities conducted propaganda in Borysław for volunteers to go to work in the Don Basin area. Indeed, groups of young Jews answered this call, but within a short time most of them returned to Borysław, after the working conditions there proved to be very harsh and did not match the promises.

The Jewish youth of Borysław integrated into all the educational institutions in the town and even went out to study in institutions of higher education in central cities of the region. A school was opened in Borysław whose language of instruction was Yiddish.

Private Jewish commerce contracted greatly, and only a few retail merchants continued to maintain their shops; these buckled under the burden of taxes. The craftsmen were, for the most part, organized in cooperatives. All the Jews who were ejected from the private economic systems found their place in municipal state services, in a consumer and marketing network, and in plants connected with the extraction of oil and the processing of ozokerite. The Jews' share was prominent in the educational institutions and in the cultural life of the town. The municipal orchestra and the music school were run mainly by Jews.

In religious life, no special disturbances were felt. Officially, the synagogues continued to exist, and a religious Jewish public filled them, especially on the Jewish festivals. The Jewish public also organized itself unofficially for the purpose of Jewish religious-communal services, such as baking matzot for Passover, the continued upkeep of the cemetery, ritual baths (*mikva'ot*), and a Jewish bathhouse. The orphanage and the Jewish kindergartens became general, mixed institutions.

When the war between Germany and the Soviet Union broke out on 22 June 1941, young Jews were called up to the Red Army; but owing to the rapid collapse of the front and the approach of the Germans, only some of them were conscripted and managed to retreat eastward. Groups of Jews — mostly from among those who had been active in the institutions of the Soviet government — also joined the evacuating Russian administration officials.

The town was captured by the Germans on 1 July 1941. That same day, the bodies of prisoners who had been murdered before the retreat were discovered in the building of the Soviet police. The Ukrainians claimed that the Jews had collaborated with the Soviet government and that they must therefore be avenged for the murder of the prisoners. The following day, the Ukrainian peasants from the area gathered in the town together with the local Ukrainians, joined by groups from among the Polish population. These, with the active assistance of the German soldiers, brutally murdered about 300 Jews in the streets of the town and wounded many dozens. In addition, the rioters broke into Jewish homes and looted property.

A few days after the pogrom, the Judenrat was established. It was headed by Michael Herz, who had not been involved in public life before the war. In its early days, the Judenrat dealt with supplying people for forced labor, with distributing the meager food rations allotted to the Jewish population, with collecting contributions imposed on the community, and with organizing material aid for the needy.

In order to recruit people for forced labor, the Judenrat divided the town into zones. At the head of each zone stood a person in charge, who compiled lists of those obligated and able to go out to work. The zone supervisors were assigned the task of setting the work rotation, and indeed things were organized this way, although there were complaints that, in exchange for a bribe, one could be released from going out to forced labor.

In August 1941 the Jewish police was established as one of the Judenrat's departments. Its first commander was Jonas, formerly a reserve officer in the Polish army.

In the months of August–October 1941, many hundreds of the Jews of Borysław were employed in forced labor, mainly in rebuilding the plants, bridges and roads that had been destroyed by the Soviets during their retreat. These labors were accompanied by acts of abuse. While working on one of the bridges in the town, the Germans and the Ukrainian policemen forced several hundred Jews to jump from it into the waters of the river. Since the water at that spot was shallow, this ended in the death of many of those who jumped.

On 29–30 November 1941, the first mass execution took place. The Germans and the Ukrainian policemen gathered up, according to a list prepared in advance by the local Ukrainians, about 1,500 Jews. These were taken to a forest near the town and executed by shooting beside pits that had been dug a few days before the massacre. One of the killing sites was the forest beyond the suburb of Tustanowice, on the road to the nearby resort town — Truskawiec. Among the victims of the *Aktion*, prominent was the number of families one of whose members had been active during the Soviet period of 1939–1941 and who were considered sympathizers of communism. In addition to these, among the victims were educated people and public figures.

In the autumn of 1941, abductions to the labor camps in the area — at Popiele, Skole and Stryj — began. At first the Judenrat tried to find young people who were willing to go out to the camps, but after reports arrived of the harsh conditions in these camps and of the loss of life, people evaded going out to the camps, and the Judenrat in Borysław faced great difficulties in filling the required quotas; for this purpose it was forced to use the Jewish police in order to comply with the Germans' demands.

The winter of 1941–1942 struck the Jews of Borysław hard. Hunger and disease felled many of them. Hundreds were infected with spotted typhus. The sick were isolated in their apartments, and medical and economic aid was extended to them. But because of the scope of the epidemic, only a little could be done to ease the condition of the sufferers. The social department attached to the Judenrat, and a branch of the JSS (Jewish Social Self-Help), whose regional center was in Lwów, organized public kitchens that distributed, day after day, hundreds of portions of hot soup to the needy.

In the spring of 1942, the Jews of Borysław initiated the establishment of various workshops, in order to secure permits that would afford a measure of immunity from deportation to the labor camps. Thus, for example, a number of large carpentry workshops were set up on Drohobyczka Street, which produced and supplied furniture to German officers who lived in the town.

At the end of July 1942, the members of the Judenrat were required to prepare lists in advance of a "transfer" of a large number of Jews from the town. The members of the Judenrat were told that those transferred would be moved eastward, but among the Jews of Borysław a suspicion lurked that this was a deportation that harbored grave dangers. A debate was conducted in the Judenrat over whether to comply with the Germans' demands and prepare lists. Among those opposed to fulfilling the German order was the Judenrat member, the engineer Elsner, and he was among the first victims of the *Aktion* that began in the days that followed. The Germans no longer waited to receive the lists of candidates for deportation and launched the *Aktion* on 4 August 1942. The Germans and the Ukrainian policemen broke into the Jews' homes and took them out to the concentration ground near the railway station. On 5 August 1942 the Germans endeavored to create the impression that the *Aktion* had ended, but in this they deceived the Jews of Borysław, who came out of their hiding places. Then they launched a new manhunt, and by the conclusion of the *Aktion* on 6 August 1942, a total of about 5,000 people had been gathered up and sent to extermination at the Bełżec camp.

After the deportation, 2 separate ghettos were established in Borysław in two neighborhoods: Potok and Wisipi. These neighborhoods were inhabited mostly by the common poor, and the housing and sanitary conditions in them were extremely harsh. Within a few days, the non-Jewish population was ordered to vacate these neighborhoods in order to allow the Jews to enter. The ghettos were open and unfenced, and the overcrowding in them was very great.

In September 1942, the chairman of the Judenrat, Michael Herz, fled and hid with non-Jewish acquaintances, and Goldman was appointed in his place. At that time there were also departures from among the Jewish police. Many Jewish policemen, who during the August *Aktion* had been forced to accompany the Germans and the Ukrainian police in searching for sites where Jews were hiding, decided to abandon the police. A central place in the Jewish police was taken at that time by Walek Eisenstein, a man considered by the members of the community to be an adventurer. He took control, with German support, of the Jewish police, and afterward imposed his authority on the Judenrat as well.

In October 1942 there was another *Aktion*, and about 1,500 Jews were sent to Bełżec. After this *Aktion* the ghettos were sealed and leaving their bounds was forbidden. In parallel with this, the Jewish workers employed in the oil industry were taken out of both parts of the ghetto and concentrated in a separate labor camp in the town. Most of them worked for the "Karpathen Öl A.G." concern.

At the beginning of November 1942, an *Aktion* began in both parts of the ghetto, which continued without interruption for about 4 weeks. Groups of Jews were concentrated in the "Coloseum" cinema hall, which was located outside the area of the ghetto, where a selection was carried out; those fit for work were separated out, and the elderly, the women and the children were sent to extermination at Bełżec. Small groups from among the deportees were sent to the Janowska camp, and several dozen Jews were murdered in the town itself. At the conclusion of this *Aktion*, the number of victims in Borysław totaled about 2,000 Jews.

On 16–17 February 1943, about 600 Jews from the ghetto were executed by shooting in mass pits near the town's slaughterhouse. In March 1943, only groups of craftsmen, persons in professions essential to the German economy, and the remnants of their families, remained in the ghetto. These were concentrated in a few houses, and every morning they went out to their workplaces under heavy escort of Ukrainian policemen. In addition, there were still about 1,200 people in the labor camp in the town. From April 1943, executions of the remnant of the ghetto residents began on a regular basis, and the slaughter continued until the end of June 1943. At that time the ghetto was effectively liquidated. The last essential workers were transferred to the labor camp in the town.

With the acceleration of the extermination process, and especially after the *Aktion* in August 1942, the Jews of Borysław intensified their efforts at rescue. Groups of young people and individuals tried to reach the Hungarian border in order to cross into that country, where, for the time being, the situation of the Jews was relatively comfortable. But most of them were caught on the way, following denunciations and surrenders by the peasants in the area. Some were murdered by the peasants, and some were handed over to the Germans, and in this way met their death. The Jews also began to build hiding places in the ghetto itself, and mostly in the surrounding forests. The Jews who worked at the oil plants scattered near the forest exploited this work in order to dig bunkers and prepare stocks of food in them. Indeed, in the final stages of the ghetto's existence, and also after it was liquidated in June 1943, these people began to hide in the bunkers and to dwell in them on a permanent basis. Only liaisons, who saw to food and water, would go out to the town and to the labor camp where Jews still remained. But the Germans and the Ukrainians conducted a systematic and constant manhunt for those in hiding, and from the middle of 1943 until the liberation of the town in August 1944, they discovered most of those in hiding and murdered them. In the liquidation of the Jews in the forests, the Ukrainian nationalist "Bandera" gangs took part, who operated in the forests in the Borysław area in the first half of 1944.

In April 1944 the Germans liquidated the labor camp in the town, and on 13 April 1944 about 600 Jews were transferred to the Płaszów camp. In May and June, additional groups of the camp's people were evacuated from Borysław to Płaszów and to the Mauthausen camp. On 21 July 1944 the last group from the camp in Borysław was sent to Auschwitz.

In Borysław there were manifestations of organization for resistance against the Nazis and their helpers among the people of the labor camp. One of the resistance cells was headed by L. Hoffmann. He was killed while attempting to strike, with his weapon, one of the camp's senior Germans. Among the groups that hid in the forests, too, there were Jews equipped with weapons, who used them against their pursuers.

The town was liberated by the Soviets on 7 August 1944, and about 200 survivors who emerged from their hiding places gathered in it. Within a few months, about 200 more Jews arrived in the town — from among those who had spent the war in the Soviet Union, as well as the survivors who returned from the Nazi camps. The remnant of the Jewry of Borysław now began to commemorate the memory of the victims; they fenced off the execution sites in the town and its surroundings, and searched for collaborators with the Germans from among the local population in order to bring them to justice. In 1945–1946, most of the community's survivors left the town and went out to Poland, and from there continued on their way to the Land of Israel or to other countries. In the 1950s and 1960s there were several hundred Jews in Borysław, but most were not natives of the place.
